import numpy as np

# Columns that should become numeric
numeric_cols = [
    "PPP",
    "FG%",
    "3FG%",
    "EFG%",
    "FT%",
    "AST_TO"
]

# Clean and convert
for col in numeric_cols:
    
    # convert to string first
    df[col] = df[col].astype(str)
    
    # remove percent signs
    df[col] = df[col].str.replace("%", "", regex=False)
    
    # replace dash placeholders with NaN
    df[col] = df[col].replace("-", np.nan)
    
    # convert to numeric
    df[col] = pd.to_numeric(df[col], errors="coerce")

# OPTIONAL:
# if you want percentages as decimals instead of whole numbers
percent_cols = ["FG%", "3FG%", "EFG%", "FT%"]

df[percent_cols] = (df[percent_cols] / 100).round(3)

# check result

# --------------------------------------------------
# TEST MULTIPLE K VALUES

inertia = []
sil_scores = []

k_values = range(2, 9)

for k in k_values:
    model = KMeans(n_clusters=k, random_state=42, n_init=20)
    labels = model.fit_predict(X_scaled)

    inertia.append(model.inertia_)
    sil_scores.append(silhouette_score(X_scaled, labels))

# --------------------------------------------------
# VISUALIZE ELBOW + SILHOUETTE

fig, ax = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(8,4))

ax[0].set_title("Elbow Method")
ax[0].set_xlabel("Number of Clusters")
ax[0].set_ylabel("Inertia")

ax[1].plot(k_values, sil_scores, marker="o")
ax[1].set_title("Silhouette Scores")
ax[1].set_xlabel("Number of Clusters")
ax[1].set_ylabel("Score")

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

KEY MODEL TAKEAWAYS
======================================================================
1. The D1 vs Non-D1 comparison chart showed that the gaps in performance were marginal. Therefore making accurate predictions is inherently difficult, especially with noisy developmental pathways and limited sample sizes.
2. This dataset doesn't account for FIBA players who never made it to the NCAA, which likely introduces survivorship bias and limits the ability to fully capture the translation process.
3. This dataset doesn't account for FIBA players who went to other international leagues or professional paths, which may limit the generalizability of the findings to all youth international players.
4. This dataset doesn't account for players who played junior college (JUCO) or Division II, which may limit the ability to capture all developmental pathways to D1.
5. This dataset looks at FIBA tournament stats which is generally 6 to 8 games per player, which is a small sample size and may not fully capture a player's true ability or potential.
6. Raw production metrics consistently outperformed efficiency metrics as predictors of future NCAA success.
7. Rebounding and scoring volume were the strongest recurring indicators across both regression and classification models.
8. Model A (Stats + Context) consistently outperformed Model B (Stats Only), showing that contextual variables meaningfully improve player evaluation.
9. Country added predictive value beyond traditional box score statistics alone.
10. The feature importance charts showed TOT_REB and PTS as dominant predictors across nearly every modeling approach.
11. Efficiency statistics such as PPP, 3PT%, and FT% had significantly lower predictive importance than expected.
12. The NCAA impact tier heatmap showed a strong progression pattern: higher-impact players consistently produced more points, rebounds, assists, and steals/blocks at the FIBA level.
13. The distribution of league_adj_best was highly right-skewed, with most players clustering in the low-to-mid impact range and a smaller subset reaching elite outcomes.
14. This high-variance outcome structure likely contributed to the moderate regression performance, as predicting exact long-term NCAA impact from youth international data is inherently noisy.
15. The classification pipeline produced stronger and more stable results than the regression models, suggesting that identifying future NCAA players is more tractable than predicting exact collegiate impact levels.
16. Recall-optimized classification models were particularly valuable because they prioritized identifying future D1 players, even at the cost of additional false positives.
17. From a scouting perspective, missing a future high-level player is often more costly than over-identifying a prospect, making recall optimization strategically important.
18. The regression models captured broad relationships between international youth production and eventual NCAA impact level, even with noisy developmental pathways.
19. Overall, the models suggest that early international production profiles contain meaningful long-term signal when paired with contextual information.
